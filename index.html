Core Frameworks & Theories

Click each framework to expand the full breakdown. Know these inside out.

This framework explains how oppression becomes normalized and internalized across generations — and how social workers and clients are positioned within it.

  1. The Beginning Born into existing power structures with no choice in social identities or starting positions. Already embedded in systems of advantage/disadvantage.
  2. First Socialization Parents and family teach roles, norms, expectations based on identities — including beliefs about food, need, and who "deserves" help.
  3. Institutional Socialization Schools, media, and religion reinforce and legitimize these power dynamics — normalizing food insecurity as a personal failure.
  4. Enforcements Society rewards conformity and punishes those who challenge the status quo. SNAP stigma is a social enforcement of the current order.
  5. The Choice Maintain status quo and benefit from privilege — OR actively interrupt the cycle through consciousness, resistance, and coalition-building.

Social workers must: examine positionality, challenge "deservingness" assumptions, redistribute decision-making power, interrupt oppressive norms within the agency.

Clients must be supported to: name struggles as systemic (not personal), exercise self-determination, engage without shame, build collective consciousness.

AOP acknowledges power imbalances and actively works to reduce them. Unlike traditional approaches that position social workers as neutral experts, AOP demands ongoing self-reflection.

  1. Full Participation Service users must be positioned as full participants in all decisions — not merely recipients of expert interventions. Are food-insecure community members co-designing programs, or just receiving services?
  2. Egalitarian Language Language must be respectful, accessible, and empowering. "Let's figure this out together" removes professional hierarchy. Avoid jargon that reinforces power.
  3. Alternative Healing Actively encourage and validate alternative healing perspectives, including cultural and spiritual approaches outside Western models. Community kitchens, mutual aid, cultural food traditions are legitimate.
  4. Just Relationships Move from Expert/Client dynamics toward genuine partnerships where power is shared. Are you an expert dispensing food — or a partner building power?

AOP Strategies: Transparency (name your power), Shared Decision-Making, Advocacy, Accountability, Continuous Learning, Collective Action.

Critical Historical Lesson (Park, 2008): Social workers who focused on "adjustment" rather than resistance facilitated the WWII incarceration of Japanese Americans. Good intentions ≠ ethical action.

Community organizing is a process where people who share a common problem come together into an organization that acts in their shared self-interest. The goal: shift power to communities — not just fix one problem, but build the capacity to solve future problems.

  1. Assessment Who is the community? What is the shared problem? Who is already affected? Who has tried to solve this before?
  2. Research Who holds the power to make change? What are their interests and vulnerabilities? What pressure points exist?
  3. Mobilization Getting people to show up. Building a base of active, committed participants.
  4. Action Targeted tactics that apply pressure to the decision-maker. Direct action, media campaigns, negotiation.
  5. Reflection Ensures the community retains and grows power after each fight. What worked? What didn't? How do we build capacity?

The Organizer's Role: You do NOT lead meetings, decide strategy, take credit, or speak for the community. You DO identify natural leaders, develop their capacity, and hold space for collective decision-making. You are NOT the hero.

Power Analysis — Key Questions: Who is the primary target (person who can make the decision)? Who is the secondary target (those who influence the decision-maker)? Who are allies, opponents, and undecided actors?

Key distinction: Community development builds assets (e.g., creates a community garden). Community organizing mobilizes residents to demand the garden — shifting who controls decisions.

The 7 Ps of Effective Policy Advocacy: Passion (personal commitment sustains you), Perseverance (policy change is a marathon), Policies (master technical details), Partners (build coalitions — no one succeeds alone), Politics (understand power dynamics), Patience (play the long game), Plan (map your theory of change first).

Strategic Steps (Conley & Jaffe):

  1. Identify the Problem Clearly define the issue affecting your community.
  2. Research the Issue Gather data, precedents, and evidence.
  3. Map Stakeholders Identify supporters and detractors.
  4. Form a Coalition Include clients and affected community members.
  5. Brand the Issue Craft memorable framing and messaging.
  6. Know Your Audience Research voting history, donors, stated values. Find your "hook" — fairness, fiscal responsibility, deservingness — that resonates with THIS target.
  7. Combine Data & Stories Data legitimizes; stories humanize. Neither alone is sufficient.
  8. Launch, Pressure, Monitor Media campaigns, grassroots mobilization, accountability tracking.

The welfare state: a framework of public programs designed to shield citizens from social risks and ensure conditions for a dignified life. In the U.S., it is uneven, fragmented, and ideologically contested.

Key Categories:

  • Social Insurance: Social Security, Medicare, Unemployment Insurance (near-universal)
  • Means-Tested Assistance: SNAP, TANF, SSI, WIC, Medicaid (income/asset tests required)
  • Housing Support: Public housing, Section 8, homelessness services
  • Family & Child: Child welfare, child care subsidies, Head Start
  • Tax-Based (Hidden): EITC, Child Tax Credit, mortgage interest deduction
  • Labor & Emergency: Minimum wage laws, FEMA/Disaster SNAP

Key Tensions to Know:

  1. "Deserving" vs. "Undeserving" Work requirements, drug testing, asset limits reflect moral judgments about who has "earned" help — echoes of English Poor Law 1601.
  2. Visible vs. Hidden Benefits SNAP recipients carry a visible card. Mortgage deductions and 401(k) subsidies are invisible. Who benefits from that difference in visibility — and who is stigmatized?
  3. Universal vs. Means-Tested Means-testing creates stigma and enrollment barriers. Those who need help most may not access it.
  4. Access Depends on Status Work history, age, citizenship, family composition all gate access — leaving many in the cracks by design.
  5. Where the Gaps Remain Food insecurity, housing instability, unmet mental health needs persist despite existing programs.

Culturally Responsive Practice Across Stages:

  • Engagement: Use preferred names, pronouns, titles. Ask "Help me understand" instead of assuming.
  • Assessment: Ask about family structure/decisions, support networks, acculturation tensions, health/healing beliefs. Go beyond intake forms.
  • Intervention Paradox: Many EBPs built for White, middle-class samples. Use culturally validated EBPs or tailor carefully. But remember: culture shapes groups, every client is different.

The Culture Iceberg: Surface = food traditions, dress, celebrations (visible). Deep = beliefs about who prepares food, what "health" means, shame around accepting charity, distrust of government programs. Culturally responsive assessment must go below the surface.

Intersectionality (Crenshaw): Social identities — race, class, gender, ability, immigration status — do not exist in isolation. They overlap and interact, creating compound, unique experiences of oppression.

Applied to food insecurity: A single undocumented mother of color faces overlapping barriers: immigration status limits SNAP eligibility, race shapes neighborhood disinvestment, gender shapes wage gaps and unpaid care work. Ignoring intersectionality means programs miss the most vulnerable.

Positionality: Your race, class, and professional credentials shape what you assume about clients. If you grew up food-secure, what assumptions do you carry into a needs assessment? How do you hold power over someone's SNAP eligibility? Positionality isn't about guilt — it's about awareness and accountability.

Implicit Bias: Unconscious mental associations that affect understanding, actions, and decisions — operating below conscious awareness. Even well-intentioned social workers harbor biases about race, class, gender, ability, and "compliance." Having biases doesn't make us bad — it makes us human. But unexamined bias causes real harm. The path: honest self-examination, regular supervision, accountability.

The Myth of Neutrality: When a social worker helps a family navigate SNAP without questioning why SNAP is so difficult to navigate — are they neutral? Or are they helping a broken system function more smoothly, thereby maintaining it?

"When we claim neutrality, we align ourselves with the powerful by default."

Neutrality is not a safe position. It is a political choice — one that preserves existing structures and the inequalities embedded within them. Every interaction involves power.

Structural Competency (Chambers & Ratliff, 2019): Shift from focusing solely on individual choices and cultural differences to examining the systemic barriers that constrain people's options.
